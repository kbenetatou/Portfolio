Designing for Food and Feelings

Why It Matters

Most diet content promotes restriction over wellness. This platform reimagines healthy eating with mental health at its core, emphasizing user empowerment and clarity.

  • Focus: Healthy eating + mental health
  • Audience: People seeking sustainable, non-triggering nutrition info
  • Goal: A structured, supportive, user-friendly experience

What I’d do differently: Partner earlier with mental health experts to align UX decisions with ethical guidelines.

Research

  • Interviews with a psychologist, nutritionist, and health-focused student
  • Competitive analysis (e.g., BBC Food)
  • Created a domain model using Brown’s conceptual modeling framework

What I’d do differently: I’d prioritize involving users with varied relationships to food and mental health to better understand their unique perspectives.

Structure

  • Hybrid card sorting of recipes and mental health content
  • Tree testing led to label refinements: e.g., “Cuisines” instead of “Healthy Flavors by Cuisine”
  • Simplified, supportive labels: “Related Recipes,” “Today’s Dinner Idea”

What I’d do differently: Run more validation rounds and involve a content strategist early on.

Flow

  • Tasks based on real user goals: finding recipes, exploring mental health topics
  • Flowchart included user thought bubbles for clarity
  • Avoided dead ends and supported both browsing and goal-based use

What I’d do differently: Co-design flows with developers to ensure technical feasibility.

Wireframes

Screens included:

  • Homepage / Main Nav
  • Recipe page with filters
  • Single Recipe View
  • Mental Health Subpage

Refinements:

  • Switched filters to a vertical list for usability
  • Adjusted navigation and labels after testing
  • 3 users tested key tasks and gave feedback

What I’d do differently: Test with a broader audience and validate with live prototype analytics.
